September 27, 1988, Tuesday, City Edition

SECTION: METRO/REGION; Pg. 62

LENGTH: 537 words

HEADLINE: Scholarships serve as memorial to bartender Friends of Abel Harris say fund's aid to the needy eases pain of his senseless slaying

BYLINE: By Beth Daley, Contributing Reporter

BODY:
It took one painful year, but friends of an Allston bartender who was murdered when he tried to calm an unruly customer said yesterday they found a sensible response to that senseless slaying.

Nothing can bring back Abel Harris, they said of their friend who was 23 years old when he was killed that hot August night last year at Bunratty's.

But yesterday Harris' friends awarded two scholarships in his name to students at Youth Enrichment Services in Boston. Harris worked as a volunteer at the nonprofit organization, which provides disadvantaged youths with sports and educational opportunities, he had spent much of his childhood there.

"There has to be something positive out of that tragedy," said George Adair, a close friend of Harris' who worked with him at Bunratty's and at YES. "You can't just push it aside." He said the scholarships are "the best possible thing that could happen out of something that bad. Abel always wanted to go to college. He would be proud of what is going on today."

A $ 1,500 scholarship went to Derek Fowler of Mattapan, a 19-year-old engineering technology student at Northeastern University. A $ 500 scholarship was awarded to Leslie Colon, 18, of Cambridge, who is attending Fitchburg State College and plans a career in law .

The scholarships came from interest earned on the $ 25,000 raised at music benefits organized by a group called Friends of Abel.

"I didn't think we'd make that much," said Dave Giammatteo, a former Bunratty's manager. "When we looked in our pockets after we first tried to raise money and saw how much was there and how much people cared, we kept on going."

James Wallace, 32, received a first-degree murder conviction in Harris' slaying and is serving a life sentence in the state prison in Walpole. Wallace's attorney, John McBride, said he is appealing the conviction based on alleged inconsistencies in witness testimony.

At Wallace's trial, witnesses testified that he was thrown out of Bunratty's for harassing customers earlier in the evening of Aug. 1, 1987. Wallace later slipped back into the bar and fired three random shots at the ceiling, witnesses said.

Harris then approached Wallace with his arms raised and tried to calm him, according to testimony. As Harris got closer to Wallace, who was on parole at the time for a robbery conviction, Wallace placed the gun at Harris' right temple and fired, witnesses said.

Harris died nine days later in St. Elizabeth's Hospital.

It is Harris' efforts at harmony and openness that his friends said they want to keep alive.

"Abel was always helping out, was a good guy. Everyone knew him, he was so outgoing and friendly. He'd help anyone," said Rich Williams, director of YES, who knew Harris for 10 years.

Williams said YES plans to expand the scholarship program to benefit more students.

Fowler, one of the scholarship recipients, said he has fond memories of repairing skis with Harris in the basement of YES, which is located on Massachusetts Avenue near the Back Bay T station.

"He was always outgoing, always helping people out. I feel a real responsibility to his name and to that philosophy," Fowler said. "I'm going to keep his name alive."
