December 28, 1989, Thursday, City Edition

HEADLINE: Active club scene meets challenge;
NIGHTLIFE

BYLINE: By Steve Morse, Globe Staff

BODY:
Nightclubs often had a tough time in the '80s. Public tastes changed. Home entertainment cut into the market. Liquor liability insurance rose dramatically. And many civic groups started to twist the knife and hope that clubs would move elsewhere because they were a "bad influence" in their neighborhoods.

It's a miracle during the conservative '80s that local clubs survived as well as they did. The decade was a volatile one, but Boston still has an active, if not thriving, live music scene. And while were many losses, there were also many gains as the city kept its world-class club image, especially for rock 'n' roll.

The best new entries? You'd have to mention Citi, which received a million-dollar facelift from its days as the Metro. Its sight lines, sound system and staging were all improved. And you'd have to mention Nightstage, the Cambridge club that was too upscale for some tastes, but still had an integrated entertainment roster which was hard to beat - from blues, jazz, rock, soul and World Music.

In fact, eclecticism, rather than strict specialization, became the rule in the '80s. Another to follow this path successfully was the Channel, which added Third World bookings, especially reggae and African acts, to its rock 'n' roll base. The Paradise Theater, still a leading rock showcase, also tried country music and jazz. And then there was Johnny D's, a former Somerville country music bar which became a funkier version of Nightstage. It also reached out to different audiences, including acoustic fans at their Tuesday night folk series.

Acoustic music was also tried at such rock clubs as the Rat, Green Street Station and T.T. the Bear's in Cambridge.

"I see club audiences as more open than they were just a few years ago," says Michael Burbank, co-owner of the Tam in Brookline, another eclectic room. "Now you can have two or three acts on a bill, and even if they play much different styles, the crowd will listen to them. That might not have happened a few years ago."

Many clubs hung tough in the '80s - the bluesy Ed Burke's, the folksy Passim Coffeehouse and the rockish Bunratty's, which endured the tragic shooting death of bartender Abel Harris by a patron. Spurred by community support, Bunratty's again became a training ground for the city's hard-rock talent.

Clubowner Pat Lyons was the decade's chief mover and shaker. He made things happen at Citi, Venus de Milo, Zanzibar and Axis, a futuristic club renamed from its Spit days.

New clubs added vitality, especially the Gallery, a sophisticated entry that spiced the black and Latin dance scenes; and the Hub Club, a new wave room for the cognoscenti.

The losses were greatest in Cambridge, where Jack's, Jonathan Swift's, the Inn Square Men's Bar and the 1369 Club bit the dust. Alas, some neighborhood types, who were obsessed with gentrification, weren't too sorry to these clubs go.

But there were positive Cambridge developments. T.T. the Bear's became a wonderfully intimate rock club. The Middle East Cafe added zany music nights. The Regattabar and Scullers added top-notch jazz. Cantares triumphed with Latin music. The Western Front scored with reggae. And yes, the Plough & Stars made it through another eccentric decade unscathed. All in all, the '80s were still a time for nightlife highs, as long as you drank responsibly, or took a cab home.
