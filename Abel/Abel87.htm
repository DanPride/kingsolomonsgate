August 3, 1987, Monday, City Edition

HEADLINE: Gang of robbers makes off with $ 29,000 at N.Y. hotels

DATELINE: NEW YORK; PORT TOWNSEND, Wash.; BOSTON; STATE COLLEGE, Pa.; WASHINGTON

BODY:
 NEW YORK - A gang of well-dressed, French-speaking robbers is believed    to be responsible for two holdups at posh midtown hotels, police said    Sunday. The first robbery occurred about 8:35 p.m. Saturday at the    Plaza Hotel on Fifth Avenue and the second about an hour later at the    Hilton Hotel on Sixth Avenue, according to police. In each robbery, the    three men and a woman followed couples from a hotel elevator to the    victims' rooms, where they forced their way inside and tied the victims    up. In the two robberies, $ 1,900 in cash and more than $ 27,000 in    jewelry were stolen, police said.
 

 
Pub patron wounds bartender   
 
BOSTON - A man thrown out of a pub returned to the establishment early    Sunday and shot and gravely wounded a bartender in front of 200    patrons, police said. The gunman fled in a car and remained at large    Sunday. The victim, Abel Harris Jr., 23, of Boston, underwent surgery    to remove bullets and bone fragments lodged in the right side of his    brain, and was listed in critical condition. Witnesses said Harris    vaulted over the bar and attempted to calm the man, but was shot twice      in the head at close range.
 

 
Divers hope to recover safe   
 
PORT TOWNSEND, Wash. - Divers hoped to recover a purser's safe Sunday    from a luxury ship that sank in 1921 believed to contain $ 1.5-million    in jewels and cash. Divers found the safe late Saturday, said Michael    Lockwood, a partner in the group trying to salvage valuables from the    SS Governor. As much as $ 8.5-million in valuables and material are    believed to remain in the hull, according to a salvage official.
 

 
System helps quiet submarines   
 
STATE COLLEGE, Pa. - A computerized inspection system will help the    Navy make quieter, harder-to-find submarines by improving propeller    design, officials say. The Intelligent Robot Inspection System, or    IRIS, developed at Pennsylvania State University, will measure the    shape of propellers more precisely and more quickly than before, said    Norman Overway, a civilian in the Navy's Submarine Propulsor Branch.    "We consider it a significant advance," Overway said recently. Making    quieter propellers is important because sound is the principle method    of detecting and locating submarines, he said.
 
 
 
Navy returning to steel   
 
WASHINGTON - Breaking a trend that began after World War II, the Navy    is building ships with steel rather than aluminum superstructures, in    part because the lighter metal was found to crack, officers say.    "Aluminum cracks at a much lower stress than steel. It doesn't have    the inherent strength," said Rear Adm. Myron Ricketts.
